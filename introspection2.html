Finding an Equilibrium Between Living Proactively and Switching "off"

  • Writer:

    Ilaria Obata

  • 01 May 2021

By unlearning one's own ‘normative’ life patterns through this global pandemic, and understanding the nuances of our relational dynamics, this year has helped us gain clarity around our ever changing reality, as we had to come to terms with how our well being can fluctuate so easily and readily depending on each day of the past year. As I come to terms with what this year long pandemic meant for me, I have to acknowledge my own positionality and privilege, knowing I had a house to continue living in, and friends and family there to support me whatever the circumstances.

As I continued writing my MA thesis during the many lockdowns that happened, Lisa Bowleg’s article ‘We’re not all in this together’, led me to further understand the opposing effects of this global lockdown. This article resonated with a number of readers, seeing as Bowleg states that throughout history, these situations of global unrest and anxiety caused by a pandemic have produced statements of solidarity and uplifting claims, such as ‘we’re all in this together’ - when in reality “we could maintain the inequitable status quo and acknowledge “we’re all in this together” for what it is: another hollow platitude of solidarity designed to placate the privileged and temporarily uncomfortable and inconvenienced” (Bowleg, 2020). Understanding the historical and statistical nuances around this term of solidarity also led me to approach these times of introspection in an extremely realistic way, knowing how polarized the effects of this pandemic have become. My own experiences, have ultimately been moulded and influenced by my own positionality, which is ultimately one of privilege; as like many others, I thought of new hobbies to pick up during the lockdown, what to cook and bake, and how to keep up with friends and family during zoom calls and quizzes.

However, feelings of anxiety and loneliness were rife for countless family and friends during this year - as my elders had to isolate completely by themselves, following the strict measures of what the lockdown entailed - unable to spend time with anyone for months on end, and solely seeing faces across a computer screen. Hence, with this March 2021 marking a year since the first lockdown in the Netherlands and across Europe, we wanted to give thought and consideration to what the past 12 months have meant for us, our writing, our growth, and more importantly our relational dynamics. Moving through this restless, yet simultaneously static, time I’ve understood not only my position, as Bowleg refers to within this system of “power and inequality which is structured for different groups”, but the importance and focality we have to place on unlearning these entrenched logics of ‘optimism and positivity’ (Bowleg, 2020). Because if anything this past year has taught me that 2021 will not necessarily bring positive change and prosperity and all those other mantras for everyone, but it's just a continuation of the now, albeit with a different number. So instead of focusing on the what ifs, and constantly thinking about the next steps, I am just trying to identify the dials I can regulate myself; which ones I can direct, and knowing that I have the agency to actually do so. The agency to resist, to speak up, to know what battles are worth fighting for, and to ultimately be more present. The past year with its ongoing and relentless stream of headlines and news stories, has shown me, quite literally, that being physically or virtually here in the present is oftentimes the most critical thing to do. We are all living through times of change and continuous disquietude, and by being in the present moment (intentionally and at one's own capacity) we are able to see each turning point, each historical event happening before us. Realizing what it means to actively see, to listen more, to read, and to speak up.

Although at times, it is so easy to be readily saturated with streams of real-time information, the last months of 2021 has shown me that finding an equilibrium between planning ahead in order to escape our current reality - and also just simply switching off sometimes (both your phone, your electronic devices, and your mental capacity to carry every single news headline that pops up each morning) is not only necessary, but it has constantly reminded me of this relationality; of reminding myself to be both present and absent - and again knowing which dials I can direct myself.

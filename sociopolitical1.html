Self-improvement or Self-sabotage?

Triggerwarning

  • Contributor:

    Chairene Chidozie

  • 01 May 2021

For many, the pandemic provided us with the opportunity to take care of ourselves. As well as change our lives for the better. From venturing into a new career to adopting a healthy lifestyle, many have used this time at home to reevaluate their priorities and replace sabotaging habits with propitious ones. ‘Reinventing’ your life by making (personal/individual) changes has become quite popular during the pandemic and sharing those personal reinventions online has become a consequent effect of the public visibility these platforms provide. From drastic weight loss videos to sharing the process of great business ideas that pivoted during the pandemic; social media has become the most favoured platform for sharing success and receiving feedback, congratulations, or approval in return.

Honor Whiteman explains in MedicalNewsToday the direct impact profit-based and accessible digital social platforms have on our mental health and what makes them so addicting. The report reveals that social media can create negative behaviour patterns and influence how users perceive themselves, affecting their overall confidence. Whiteman suggests that the relational link between social media and our contemporary context are mutually constitutive with the need to belong and relate to this electronic domain, thus this platform, albeit with its pros and cons, has become the perpetrator for this addiction to wanting to 'relate and belong, to the majority (Whiteman, 2015). He further states that the rise in popularity and visibility of uploading 'fitness transformation' videos, though sometimes motivating, could stem from us wanting to mitigate negative emotions created from making romanticized comparisons with others' idealized online versions of themselves. The problem here is that the use of social media has become irrational and excessive, allowing for this comparative dynamic, between us and what we are seeing, to turn into a destructive domino effect. Thus, leading it to interfere massively with our daily lives.

We, as a society, are very visual. Hence, transformation videos with the most noticeable improvements are likely to receive the most online praise. Although online praise may be initially satisfying, the impulse and or growing inclination to amplify online/digital recognition based on the most drastic transformation journeys - through videos or pictures - can become one of the main driving factors towards self-sabotaging behaviour. For instance, taking extreme measures to achieve drastic weight loss, followed by the sharing of that achievement online with the hopes of receiving a multitude of praise. When I look at myself, there is evidence of allowing positive body transformation videos to trigger negative emotions. Those triggers eventually correlate to the growth of participants in extreme weight loss challenges. And the decline of individuals adopting more sustainable and healthy habits that may result in slow weight loss, which is more tenable both for oneself and the unlearning of these destructive weight loss habits.

Image: Love thy self or society's approval?

My approach to achieving extreme weight loss is an example of how the viral trend of sharing self-improvement videos may influence individuals to adopt an intangible lifestyle. Emphasising the importance of considering your intent before embarking on a journey that may transform your life. It is clear to me that the next time I contemplate developing a fitness mindset and changing my body, I should clarify the intentions I want to set for myself. Whether they are rooted in simply feeling better, to start eating healthier, or whether they are a product of external (societal) pressure. By clarifying my intentions, I may understand the end results better and identify the end beneficiaries. It is vital to establish the end beneficiaries of your goals as by doing so, if, for instance,if you are not one of the beneficiaries, achieving that goal may leave an unfulfilling inkling.

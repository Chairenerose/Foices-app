Introspection: Reflecting on One Year of Covid-19

Finding my inner self during the pandemic

Writer:

Chairene Chidozie

The night of December 31st 2019, was a special night for everyone. A new decade awaited us all, which many associated with new beginnings and unrealistic promises. I was heavily affected by the latter.

As I savored my prosecco rosé, I wrote a letter to my future self, where I outlined everything I wanted to accomplish by the end of 2020. What began as a waltz between the current version of myself and the powerful Michelle Obama—whom for me, like many others is a constant source of inspiration. Ended with a list of dull goals, any young lady who had been heartbroken, gained a few pounds, or a victim of consumerism could have composed. Every goal I had written had a theme of self-transformation with the intent of receiving external validation. Little did I know that my incapability of smartly setting goals would not define 2020. Instead, it would be defined by a global pandemic that not only interrupted a normative routine/lifestyle for so many, but it further amplified racial and social injustices through a racially divisive lens.

When I first caught wind of the pandemic and how my busy yet unfulfilling life was about to change drastically, I was delighted. It meant that I had an excuse to not keep up with my social circles. My relief screamed 'privileged', whilst many worried about job and food security; I embraced that the urgency of meeting my commitments were gradually fading.

This time of solitude provided by the pandemic revealed the urgency to relearn how to live for myself. Without a doubt, I believed composing a new list of goals was the best way to approach this successfully. However, this time, I decided to write down everything that made me happy and enjoy life more.

With the intent of being as honest as possible, my list illuminated that I was a food-loving, reality tv admirer who cared about making an impact on the world. I tackled this premature epitaph of myself by doing each of the things on my list within one week. Other than this approach being far from fulfilling, it was also draining and exhausting.

As I enjoyed my banana brownies and listened to a podcast by Women of Impact, I realised that the past week I had been forcing myself to do 'joyful' things, making those things draining, as it felt like quite a superficial approach to this drastic change. My unsuccessful approach did not mean that I was out of touch with my feelings; I just needed a break to breathe and come to terms with myself.

Image: My What Makes Me Happy List

It was time to integrate my essential self. The focus here was to review my life and happiness triggers. After doing so, I concluded that my 'what makes me happy' list was at surface level reflective of my “goals”, that I wanted to achieve. But looking back at it - this list like many others - reminded me of how unnecessary and how out of touch those goals were with my true feelings, as I was suffering from emotional exhaustion at the time of writing it. By forcing myself to activate my happy hormones, I was indirectly punishing myself for being sad. If this year has taught me anything, it is that it is okay not to be okay. Sometimes permitting yourself to let out your sadness is the best way to trigger your happiness.

Finding an Equilibrium Between Living proactively and "Switching Off"

Writer:

Ilaria Obata

By unlearning one's own ‘normative’ life patterns through this global pandemic, and understanding the nuances of our relational dynamics, this year has helped us gain clarity around our ever changing reality, as we had to come to terms with how our well being can fluctuate so easily and readily depending on each day of the past year. As I come to terms with what this year long pandemic meant for me, I have to acknowledge my own positionality and privilege, knowing I had a house to continue living in, and friends and family there to support me whatever the circumstances.

As I continued writing my MA thesis during the many lockdowns that happened, Lisa Bowleg’s article ‘We’re not all in this together’, led me to further understand the opposing effects of this global lockdown. This article resonated with a number of readers, seeing as Bowleg states that throughout history, these situations of global unrest and anxiety caused by a pandemic have produced statements of solidarity and uplifting claims, such as ‘we’re all in this together’ - when in reality “we could maintain the inequitable status quo and acknowledge “we’re all in this together” for what it is: another hollow platitude of solidarity designed to placate the privileged and temporarily uncomfortable and inconvenienced” (Bowleg, 2020). Understanding the historical and statistical nuances around this term of solidarity also led me to approach these times of introspection in an extremely realistic way, knowing how polarized the effects of this pandemic have become. My own experiences, have ultimately been moulded and influenced by my own positionality, which is ultimately one of privilege; as like many others, I thought of new hobbies to pick up during the lockdown, what to cook and bake, and how to keep up with friends and family during zoom calls and quizzes.

Image: Illustration by seeyousioe

However, feelings of anxiety and loneliness were rife for countless family and friends during this year - as my elders had to isolate completely by themselves, following the strict measures of what the lockdown entailed - unable to spend time with anyone for months on end, and solely seeing faces across a computer screen. Hence, with this March 2021 marking a year since the first lockdown in the Netherlands and across Europe, we wanted to give thought and consideration to what the past 12 months have meant for us, our writing, our growth, and more importantly our relational dynamics. Moving through this restless, yet simultaneously static, time I’ve understood not only my position, as Bowleg refers to within this system of “power and inequality which is structured for different groups”, but the importance and focality we have to place on unlearning these entrenched logics of ‘optimism and positivity’ (Bowleg, 2020). Because if anything this past year has taught me that 2021 will not necessarily bring positive change and prosperity and all those other mantras for everyone, but it's just a continuation of the now, albeit with a different number. So instead of focusing on the what ifs, and constantly thinking about the next steps, I am just trying to identify the dials I can regulate myself; which ones I can direct, and knowing that I have the agency to actually do so. The agency to resist, to speak up, to know what battles are worth fighting for, and to ultimately be more present. The past year with its ongoing and relentless stream of headlines and news stories, has shown me, quite literally, that being physically or virtually here in the present is oftentimes the most critical thing to do. We are all living through times of change and continuous disquietude, and by being in the present moment (intentionally and at one's own capacity) we are able to see each turning point, each historical event happening before us. Realizing what it means to actively see, to listen more, to read, and to speak up.

Although at times, it is so easy to be readily saturated with streams of real-time information, the last months of 2021 has shown me that finding an equilibrium between planning ahead in order to escape our current reality - and also just simply switching off sometimes (both your phone, your electronic devices, and your mental capacity to carry every single news headline that pops up each morning) is not only necessary, but it has constantly reminded me of this relationality; of reminding myself to be both present and absent - and again knowing which dials I can direct myself.

Self-improvement or Self-sabotage?
Triggerwarning

Writer:

Chairene Chidozie

For many, the pandemic provided us with the opportunity to take care of ourselves. As well as change our lives for the better. From venturing into a new career to adopting a healthy lifestyle, many have used this time at home to reevaluate their priorities and replace sabotaging habits with propitious ones. ‘Reinventing’ your life by making (personal/individual) changes has become quite popular during the pandemic and sharing those personal reinventions online has become a consequent effect of the public visibility these platforms provide. From drastic weight loss videos to sharing the process of great business ideas that pivoted during the pandemic; social media has become the most favoured platform for sharing success and receiving feedback, congratulations, or approval in return.

Honor Whiteman explains in MedicalNewsToday the direct impact profit-based and accessible digital social platforms have on our mental health and what makes them so addicting. The report reveals that social media can create negative behaviour patterns and influence how users perceive themselves, affecting their overall confidence. Whiteman suggests that the relational link between social media and our contemporary context are mutually constitutive with the need to belong and relate to this electronic domain, thus this platform, albeit with its pros and cons, has become the perpetrator for this addiction to wanting to 'relate and belong, to the majority (Whiteman, 2015). He further states that the rise in popularity and visibility of uploading 'fitness transformation' videos, though sometimes motivating, could stem from us wanting to mitigate negative emotions created from making romanticized comparisons with others' idealized online versions of themselves. The problem here is that the use of social media has become irrational and excessive, allowing for this comparative dynamic, between us and what we are seeing, to turn into a destructive domino effect. Thus, leading it to interfere massively with our daily lives.

We, as a society, are very visual. Hence, transformation videos with the most noticeable improvements are likely to receive the most online praise. Although online praise may be initially satisfying, the impulse and or growing inclination to amplify online/digital recognition based on the most drastic transformation journeys - through videos or pictures - can become one of the main driving factors towards self-sabotaging behaviour. For instance, taking extreme measures to achieve drastic weight loss, followed by the sharing of that achievement online with the hopes of receiving a multitude of praise. When I look at myself, there is evidence of allowing positive body transformation videos to trigger negative emotions. Those triggers eventually correlate to the growth of participants in extreme weight loss challenges. And the decline of individuals adopting more sustainable and healthy habits that may result in slow weight loss, which is more tenable both for oneself and the unlearning of these destructive weight loss habits.

Image: Love thy self or society's approval?

My approach to achieving extreme weight loss is an example of how the viral trend of sharing self-improvement videos may influence individuals to adopt an intangible lifestyle. Emphasising the importance of considering your intent before embarking on a journey that may transform your life. It is clear to me that the next time I contemplate developing a fitness mindset and changing my body, I should clarify the intentions I want to set for myself. Whether they are rooted in simply feeling better, to start eating healthier, or whether they are a product of external (societal) pressure. By clarifying my intentions, I may understand the end results better and identify the end beneficiaries. It is vital to establish the end beneficiaries of your goals as by doing so, if, for instance,if you are not one of the beneficiaries, achieving that goal may leave an unfulfilling inkling.

The Mutually Constitutive Relationship Between Hypersexualisation and Racism

Writer:

Ilaria Obata

Image: Illustration by @maaike.hartjes

Ethnic, exotic, oriental, are all terms and words that I've heard on countless occasions to describe not only Asian cuisines and dishes, but they are more prominently associated with the depiction and representation of Asian women, both onscreen and in our current contextual setting. The seminal and heavily cited work by Edward Said, has coined this form of exoticization of the East, or 'the Orient', in his work Orientalism in which he "considers Orientalism as a powerful European ideological creation - a way for writers, philosophers and colonial administrators to deal with the 'otherness' of eastern culture, customs and beliefs" (Said, 2003). Thus, giving way to contemporary racist beliefs and Othering philosophies within our contextual setting. The ‘orientalizing’ of entire people groups and countries has filtered into our everyday vernacular – when speaking about Other(s), and trying to superimpose this Orientalist ideology within the everyday.

Unfortunately, my own experiences, like so many others in my social environment, have become a by-product of this dynamic between the hyper-sexualisation of Asian females and media-generated representations of women in manga/anime that have permeated into Eurocentric media outlets and pop-culture - along with early onset depictions of Asian female characters in Hollywood, with movies such as Daughter of Dragon (1931), The World of Suzie Wong (1960), Year of the Dragon (1985).

Having moved from San Francisco to Rome during my early adolescent years, I was made so readily aware of how my physical features immediately defined me to others around me: being called cinesina (which literally translates into small Chinese girl) and constantly being reminded how 'flat' my face was, or how ‘oriental’ I looked. How I looked and sounded to the rest of the people in my classroom became so embedded in how I viewed myself, as always different to the rest of my (white) classmates, constantly exoticized through this Othering lens.

Now I’ve had the chance to speak more and more about these instances of microaggressions and racism that occured at such an early age with other friends, who had also experienced the same types of interactions. This has made me realize just how normalized this form of sexualized racism is, which led me – like others – to oftentimes suppress this responsive anger or irritation towards these comments, seeing as it felt like even if we said something back then it wouldn’t make a difference, as even the teachers in these classrooms were aware of these comments being said and still remained silent.

Immediately defining a person based on their 'oriental and exotic' features, has created demeaning and degrading language and has constructed racial tropes that have led people to think they are able to pinpoint everything about a woman solely based on the fact that 'she looks Asian'. These stereotyped tropes are so deeply imbedded in historical depictions of Asian women, and as suggested by cultural theorist and scholar Stuart Hall, these constructions of Other(ness) are heavily intertwined with identity politics, which “get hold of the few simple and easily grasped, characteristics about a person and reduce everything about the person to those traits, fixing them into eternity” (Merskin, 2009). It is precisely this notion of longevity that upholds and reinforces racial caricatures in our everyday life. This is exemplified through several North American comedy films that make fun of Asian nail salons and their employees' accents, or martial arts movies that play upon these embedded stereotypes of the 'kung fu master', or the submissive Asian sub-character. Hall further speaks about how "consistency of stereotypical portrayals is likely to add credibility to those portrayals, as they take on an aura of naturalness and truth" (Merskin, 2009). Hence, these portrayals are "constantly reified through the lack of contradictory images and information" (Merskin, 2009), present in our spaces of the everyday, as a byproduct of media-generated stereotypes.

Still shot from video by: Kyoko Takenaka (@jinjabrew)

Kyoko Takenaka is a Japanese-American musician, filmmaker, director and performer, whose work HOME speaks directly to this ongoing and normalized take on the fetishization of Asian women, by primarily white-cis men. Takenaka recorded countless conversations in bars, on dates, and in ubers with men commenting on her own heritage, her appearance, and making sweeping statements on their 'take' on Asian cultural, culinary histories, and Asian countries. She directly conveys the shared interaction we as asian women face with strangers who try to ‘flirt’ by guessing our ethnicity, or by saying that 'they just have a thing' for Asian women. These interactions are the product of centuries of degrading interpretations of East Asia and Southeast Asian (visual) culture, which has moulded sexualized representations of Asian women; depicted as cute and or submissive.

Takenaka films her blank facial expressions as she records herself listening to these audio recordings. Her building anger and frustration are all subtly shown through her blankness, the slight raising of her eyebrows, and her side eye. Her still shots not only depict just how normalized these interactions are, especially in Western Europe and North America, but they show how oftentimes these statements saying, that “I looooove asian food, but will never eat chicken feet”, generate such a disbelief upon hearing them that the rage and anger one feels towards these comments can be processed much later on. In fact, as stated by Takenaka herself “she sat there in disbelief every time digesting the audacity of these men, recording and archiving allowed me the space to know that one day, my anger and trauma would be used for something; it would not swallow or eat me whole then or now" (Takenaka, 2021).

Takenaka’s HOME video is triggering me for me, like many others, yet it can be seen as a way to convey just how intertwined racism and hyper-fetishisization are, and although it "centers Takenaka's Othering in such close proximity to whiteness” (Takenaka, 2021), this video pushes forth new types of discussions, ones of outrage and of showing just how universal these experiences can be for so many women.

Illustration by: @seeyousioe

Ultimately, I believe that we can celebrate differences (whether that be cultural, social, and or historical), as ‘difference’ is not the issue here. It is how we frame, depict, represent, and speak about differences that leads there to be such polarizing, degrading, and fetishizing representations of Asian women - through a sexual, racial, and ethnicizing lens. In order to unmake the decades of stereotyped and fetishized tropes surrounding Asian (femme) representation, we have to start creating a space for discussion: through artworks, through music, through film, in which (accessible) discussions are designed and displayed for the people and do not simply reproduce the same logic of display about Others. These conversations can acts as a crucial tool in the reimagining of a logic of representation, and of difference that discards linearity and completely unmakes a heteronormative logic of racial governance and fetishization. Organizations like @asian.raisins, @panasiancollective , @miss_zing, gall-dem, @calikyecab, Stop AAPI Hate, and AMHP amongst many others helps us all to collectively rethink a future in which racial stereotyping, hate crimes and hyper fetishization have no place to be reified within. Where our cultural and social ‘differences’ are no longer seen as a 'marker' to hyper sexualize or fetishize us.

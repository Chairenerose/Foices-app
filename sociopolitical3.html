The Story of Maluku

  • Contributor:

    Enseline Lenahan

  • 15 June 2021

As I began writing this piece, I had to reckon with an ongoing relationality between personal introspection and historical research. There is so much active oppression in the world that it makes you wonder if your cause is more important than the cause of your peers. Unfortunately, minority groups worldwide are impacted by oppression, when in fact, we all deserve the right to equality, justice, dignity and freedom. One shouldn’t have to fight for the spotlight. Nevertheless, sometimes the spotlight skips a community, which is why it is up to individuals to ensure that the stories of their people are told. This is the story of Maluku.

Image: Map of the Maluku Islands

Maluku is a hidden tropical paradise that almost seems too good to be true. Maluku is an archipelago above Australia, next to West Papua and Timor-Leste. Please note that I purposely neglected to mention Indonesia. The Maluku Islands have been considered part of Oceania’s Melanesia region. Like the other regions of Oceania, Maluku has beautiful reefs, empty stretches of powdery white sand beaches, mountains and rainforests. With the Banda Sea, Maluku has one of the deepest seas globally, more than 7,000 meters deep. The Maluku Islands are also rich in natural resources, such as gold and petrol.

The indigenous people of Maluku are called AlifURU, which means the first human (man and woman). The AlifURU come from NunuSaku - the birthplace of humankind - The first people left NunuSaku in groups, and their descendants populated all four corners of the earth. All nations evolved from the four Human Children and the four human species, the white human, the yellow human, the black human, and the brown human. So yes, we divided groups based on skin colour, but we never segregated communities based on skin colour. Translated into the contemporary, you can say that the indigenous people(s) of Maluku were the first to practice inclusivity and diversity.

Moreover, each island has maintained its traditions, beautiful tribe stories, culture and language. Our way of life is “Adat” ; There is one God, The Highest. Thus, our rights and obligations are established and regulated according to this principle. This includes the division of land, caring for our natural living environment, and keeping a balanced life. Furthermore, the Adat shows how to maintain relationships with different islands or tribe clans. Therefore, we can feel equally connected; we have always lived with diversity, peace and harmony.

Maluku was known as the Spice Islands between the 16th and 18th century. The islands were the world’s only supplier of nutmeg, cloves and mace. Back then, these spices were considered very vital and very valuable trade commodities. The search and subsequent fight for control of Maluku helped kick-start European colonialism. Maluku was first exploited by the Portuguese and then the Dutch. The arrival of the Dutch resulted in a significant alteration in the social and political landscape of Maluku.

As a result of colonialism, Maluku people know oppression like no other. That is why today, we reflect on all the injustice in the world. The world needs all the help it can to overcome the effects of centuries of inequality and oppression. That is why I am an advocate for Change The Future of Maluku . A movement to raise awareness for equality, justice, dignity and freedom for the people of Maluku.

In Maluku, women have actively pushed forth calls for change and accountability. Two of our heroes are Martha Christina Tiahahu and Hansina Uktolseja. These two women stood up against colonial oppression and aggression to free the Maluku people. To enhance awareness for women’s rights in Maluku, I began a petition for our first lady. She is considered the mother of our people. Her name is Njonja Soumokil, and she still lives in exile in The Netherlands. Her husband, Mr Dr Christiaan Soumokil, was our president and a freedom fighter. He was unlawfully executed 55 years ago by an Indonesian firing squad on the 12th of April 1966.

Image: Njonja Soumokil, widow of Dr. Christiaan Soumokil

Mrs Soumokil has been looking for his grave ever since. However, her case entirely depends on the Indonesian government's willingness to make the location of the grave public. According to a Dutch court ruling in 2018, the Dutch government is obligated to investigate the exact location of her husband's grave. In order to provide Mrs Soumokil with the exact location of her husbands grave, a petition has been made. To motivate the Dutch Government to apply more pressure on Indonesian authorities to conform to international law.

The world needs to know that colonialism did not end simply because the Dutch left. In 1949 after a war of independence, the Dutch ceded control of the archipelago. During the decolonisation process, Maluku leaders counselled the people. With the people's true will, they declared the Republik Maluku Selatan (RMS) independence on the 25th of April 1950. But this did not last long. Though the Dutch left, they left their colonial imprints behind, as their colonial roots provided the blueprint for Indonesia to take over as an oppressor. More specifically, to hold on to favourable trade relations.

Moreover, Indonesia invaded the capital island of Maluku, Ambon. Thus, the illegal annexation of the RMS became real. As a result of Indonesia's neo-colonialism, Maluku has been left in poverty and has prevented any form of development favouring the local population. Maluku is now a province of Indonesia and listed as the 4th poorest province of Indonesia's 34 provinces . Change The Future of Maluku provides support in building communication, leadership and political processes for freedom of the oppressed people of Maluku. Our goal is to provide the local population with the tools to free themselves from the Indonesian neocolonial tentacles, enabling them to determine their future.

I am here to learn, share lessons learned, overcome challenges and work together to thrive through conversation. I want the world to understand that neocolonialism still exists in various forms and locations. Furthermore, I aim to explain this by starting with the story of Maluku. We don’t have to fight over the spotlight, but we must share the spotlight to educate the world.

Together we catalyse positive change by networking and funding the youth-led community human rights movements and liberation campaigns in Maluku. Focus on changing public opinion is essential to gain the support of the international community. It takes a lot of courage to stand against the oppressor, Indonesia, knowing nobody is watching. At this moment, dozens of peaceful Maluku activists face lifetime jail sentences because they raised our flag. Razzias are held randomly by the Indonesian State Army to arrest Maluku freedom fighters. In other words, anyone who expresses a free opinion, especially if it is politically oriented, is silenced.

Image: Enseline at a Black Lives Matter Protest in Dam Square, Amsterdam 2021

Everyone has the right to life and liberty, freedom from slavery and torture, freedom of opinion and expression, the right to work and education and many more (Universal Declaration of Human Rights, UDHR). Human rights arise simply by being a human being. We are all equally entitled to our human rights without discrimination. These rights are all interrelated, interdependent and indivisible. Unfortunately, it is too difficult for the 2.1 million civilians in Maluku to speak up about it. Please help us amplify their voices. So the world can hear the untold violations of humanitarian and human rights law due to the neocolonialism by Indonesia in the annexed Republik Maluku Selatan. Through this support, the people in Maluku will be aware that they are not alone. The international community is watching and creating platforms for the people of Maluku to break their silence.

The F-OICE-S of the oppressed must be amplified to make positive changes in this beautiful world we are living in. To protect our land, oceans, animals and our people. All our people! Equally!

I want to leave you with this song Jangan putus harapanmu (don’t give up your hope). It is a song I wrote saying do not give up hope, just believe that Maluku will be free.

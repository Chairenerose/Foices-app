The Story of Maluku

Contributor:

Enseline Lenahan

As I began writing this piece, I had to reckon with an ongoing relationality between personal introspection and historical research. There is so much active oppression in the world that it makes you wonder if your cause is more important than the cause of your peers. Unfortunately, minority groups worldwide are impacted by oppression, when in fact, we all deserve the right to equality, justice, dignity and freedom. One shouldn’t have to fight for the spotlight. Nevertheless, sometimes the spotlight skips a community, which is why it is up to individuals to ensure that the stories of their people are told. This is the story of Maluku.

Image: Map of the Maluku Islands

Maluku is a hidden tropical paradise that almost seems too good to be true. Maluku is an archipelago above Australia, next to West Papua and Timor-Leste. Please note that I purposely neglected to mention Indonesia. The Maluku Islands have been considered part of Oceania’s Melanesia region. Like the other regions of Oceania, Maluku has beautiful reefs, empty stretches of powdery white sand beaches, mountains and rainforests. With the Banda Sea, Maluku has one of the deepest seas globally, more than 7,000 meters deep. The Maluku Islands are also rich in natural resources, such as gold and petrol.

The indigenous people of Maluku are called AlifURU, which means the first human (man and woman). The AlifURU come from NunuSaku - the birthplace of humankind - The first people left NunuSaku in groups, and their descendants populated all four corners of the earth. All nations evolved from the four Human Children and the four human species, the white human, the yellow human, the black human, and the brown human. So yes, we divided groups based on skin colour, but we never segregated communities based on skin colour. Translated into the contemporary, you can say that the indigenous people(s) of Maluku were the first to practice inclusivity and diversity.

Moreover, each island has maintained its traditions, beautiful tribe stories, culture and language. Our way of life is “Adat” ; There is one God, The Highest. Thus, our rights and obligations are established and regulated according to this principle. This includes the division of land, caring for our natural living environment, and keeping a balanced life. Furthermore, the Adat shows how to maintain relationships with different islands or tribe clans. Therefore, we can feel equally connected; we have always lived with diversity, peace and harmony.

Maluku was known as the Spice Islands between the 16th and 18th century. The islands were the world’s only supplier of nutmeg, cloves and mace. Back then, these spices were considered very vital and very valuable trade commodities. The search and subsequent fight for control of Maluku helped kick-start European colonialism. Maluku was first exploited by the Portuguese and then the Dutch. The arrival of the Dutch resulted in a significant alteration in the social and political landscape of Maluku.

As a result of colonialism, Maluku people know oppression like no other. That is why today, we reflect on all the injustice in the world. The world needs all the help it can to overcome the effects of centuries of inequality and oppression. That is why I am an advocate for Change The Future of Maluku . A movement to raise awareness for equality, justice, dignity and freedom for the people of Maluku.

In Maluku, women have actively pushed forth calls for change and accountability. Two of our heroes are Martha Christina Tiahahu and Hansina Uktolseja. These two women stood up against colonial oppression and aggression to free the Maluku people. To enhance awareness for women’s rights in Maluku, I began a petition for our first lady. She is considered the mother of our people. Her name is Njonja Soumokil, and she still lives in exile in The Netherlands. Her husband, Mr Dr Christiaan Soumokil, was our president and a freedom fighter. He was unlawfully executed 55 years ago by an Indonesian firing squad on the 12th of April 1966.

Image: Njonja Soumokil, widow of Dr. Christiaan Soumokil

Mrs Soumokil has been looking for his grave ever since. However, her case entirely depends on the Indonesian government's willingness to make the location of the grave public. According to a Dutch court ruling in 2018, the Dutch government is obligated to investigate the exact location of her husband's grave. In order to provide Mrs Soumokil with the exact location of her husbands grave, a petition has been made. To motivate the Dutch Government to apply more pressure on Indonesian authorities to conform to international law.

The world needs to know that colonialism did not end simply because the Dutch left. In 1949 after a war of independence, the Dutch ceded control of the archipelago. During the decolonisation process, Maluku leaders counselled the people. With the people's true will, they declared the Republik Maluku Selatan (RMS) independence on the 25th of April 1950. But this did not last long. Though the Dutch left, they left their colonial imprints behind, as their colonial roots provided the blueprint for Indonesia to take over as an oppressor. More specifically, to hold on to favourable trade relations.

Moreover, Indonesia invaded the capital island of Maluku, Ambon. Thus, the illegal annexation of the RMS became real. As a result of Indonesia's neo-colonialism, Maluku has been left in poverty and has prevented any form of development favouring the local population. Maluku is now a province of Indonesia and listed as the 4th poorest province of Indonesia's 34 provinces . Change The Future of Maluku provides support in building communication, leadership and political processes for freedom of the oppressed people of Maluku. Our goal is to provide the local population with the tools to free themselves from the Indonesian neocolonial tentacles, enabling them to determine their future.

I am here to learn, share lessons learned, overcome challenges and work together to thrive through conversation. I want the world to understand that neocolonialism still exists in various forms and locations. Furthermore, I aim to explain this by starting with the story of Maluku. We don’t have to fight over the spotlight, but we must share the spotlight to educate the world.

Together we catalyse positive change by networking and funding the youth-led community human rights movements and liberation campaigns in Maluku. Focus on changing public opinion is essential to gain the support of the international community. It takes a lot of courage to stand against the oppressor, Indonesia, knowing nobody is watching. At this moment, dozens of peaceful Maluku activists face lifetime jail sentences because they raised our flag. Razzias are held randomly by the Indonesian State Army to arrest Maluku freedom fighters. In other words, anyone who expresses a free opinion, especially if it is politically oriented, is silenced.

Image: Enseline at a Black Lives Matter Protest

Everyone has the right to life and liberty, freedom from slavery and torture, freedom of opinion and expression, the right to work and education and many more (Universal Declaration of Human Rights, UDHR). Human rights arise simply by being a human being. We are all equally entitled to our human rights without discrimination. These rights are all interrelated, interdependent and indivisible. Unfortunately, it is too difficult for the 2.1 million civilians in Maluku to speak up about it. Please help us amplify their voices. So the world can hear the untold violations of humanitarian and human rights law due to the neocolonialism by Indonesia in the annexed Republik Maluku Selatan. Through this support, the people in Maluku will be aware that they are not alone. The international community is watching and creating platforms for the people of Maluku to break their silence.

The F-OICE-S of the oppressed must be amplified to make positive changes in this beautiful world we are living in. To protect our land, oceans, animals and our people. All our people! Equally!

I want to leave you with this song Jangan putus harapanmu (don’t give up your hope). It is a song I wrote saying do not give up hope, just believe that Maluku will be free.

What It’s Like to Be Black in a Predominantly White Student Association - Jazzmin Hoepel

Contributor:

Chairene Chidozie

Dutch student associations are social organisations for Dutch college and university students. It is common for these student associations to be a home base for a collective group of sororities and fraternities. For many students, joining a student association can provide a home away from home. Thus, each year, thousands of students are extremely eager to follow a formal recruitment process in the hopes of pledging to a sorority or fraternity. Although being part of this prestigious members-only community is rewarding, the exclusiveness of this community is often misconstrued to be a social organisation perpetuating a culture of toxicity.

When one does join a student association, their friends and family are often quick to judge them, especially when that individual does not fit the description of a “typical student association” member. These initial confused and carping remarks are something Jazzmin Hoepel (23) is far too familiar with. Despite being born in Limburg to a Dutch mother and Surinamese father, she grew up in Utrecht, a part of two different worlds, growing up in a multicultural neighbourhood yet attended a posh public high school. It was clear that from a young age, Jazzmin knew how to adapt to new environments. When Jazzmin joined the student association Plato in Tilburg in 2019 and pledged to the Sorority Amendus Amandus, she had no trouble fully immersing herself in this social environment. Since then, she has joined various committees at Plato and is currently a board member of the ‘Bodemdrift Festival’ organized by Amendus Amandus. Despite the numerous opportunities provided to her, Jazzmin is often met with confusing gazes when she discloses her affiliation to student associations. This is due to the general interpretation that student associations are for “rich white families”. However, this couldn’t be further from the truth.

Plato is a place where everyone is welcome. There is not necessarily one specific demographic that makes up the majority of the association’s members. This eventually convinced Jazzmin to become a member.

“I was attracted to Plato as not everyone looked alike; in terms of colour, behaviour and physical appearances”

Image: Jazzmin at Plato in her Committee uniform

Jazzmin was confident that she would fit right into an association where differences are celebrated. Surprisingly, this overly-optimistic outlook on student associations was first ominous when her friend shared her plans on joining a student association three years ago. At that time, Jazzmin perceived student associations to be an unnecessary waste of time, where forced friendships are glorified. It follows that many share these biased perceptions of student associations. A range of factors has fuelled the construction of these stereotypes. Starting with past tragedies that highlight the dark side of these associations, to the role television series such as Feuten and Ares have played where problematic aspects of Dutch student association life is both glorified and mocked.

Furthermore, student associations are often segregated from the general community in these student cities. Thus, judgements are effortlessly made. As the exclusivity of these associations leaves the public with limited information about them. Therefore, to understand these groups, they are categorized according to the labels provided to the public. For instance, what is showcased by the media. This simplification of the unknown is what leads to the construction of generalized stigmas of Dutch student associations.

The result of this bubble that has been formed around Dutch student associations across cities in the Netherlands is that they have become a community of their own. Despite the numerous associations in multiple cities, they all understand each other to some extent. This exclusion has generated a general opinion that student associations are only for a specific race, size and class. What has further triggered this presumption is that student associations are predominantly white. Thus, associations are often accused of excluding non-white races from these societies. In addition to that, there have been numerous tragedies where students from non-white backgrounds have experienced racial discrimination within these associations. Understanding the lack of multiculturalism within these associations is not as easy as the public presumes it is.

Having been a member for more than a year, Jazzmin believes Dutch student associations are currently open to all prospective students. However, a particular demographic of students are most likely to express their interest in them. Usually, these students already know before enrolling in university that pledging to a sorority or fraternity is essential to experiencing the best type of student life. In addition, the financial position of an individual or their family also plays a role, as “joining a student association is just an expensive joke”, seeing as being part of an association can cost you hundreds of euros. This may insinuate that students who are second-generation immigrants to the Netherlands don’t necessarily have the goal of joining a student association as their number one priority during university.

“I have witnessed it myself in my neighbourhood that is predominantly inhabited with immigrants. There isn’t much talk of sororities”

This provided a sharp difference from the general conversations at her high school, where many of her classmates were impressed by student associations, probably due to the privileges of being a native. Ultimately, Jazzmin advocates that it is crucial to bear in mind that “one’s environment has a lot of influence on the choices one makes without even noticing it at that moment”.

The homophily effect clarifies why students are so often attracted to social organisations that resemble their characteristics. Being around others with similar values, attitudes, behaviours, or even being of the same race can increase one’s comfort level in social situations. This theory offers another reason as to why student associations are predominantly white. When questioned on race in student associations, Jazzmin acted in congruence with this theory,

“it just attracts you faster when you identify with the image a student association portrays”

Nonetheless, this simplified explanation of why Dutch student associations look the way they do is often not accepted by the public. Associations often face criticism for their diversity and inclusion efforts. Jazzmin vehemently disagrees; on the contrary, she believes that “it has to happen naturally” and offers another solution in which associations could dedicate more time to inclusive representation on their social media and event posters. This tactic may reach a more diverse audience, as it’s easier to identify with someone who looks like you than someone who doesn’t look like you.

Diversity is what subconsciously influenced Jazzmin to pledge to the sorority Amendus Amandus. Although she immediately clicked with the other ladies, Jazzmin has realised that seeing other ladies who looked like her may have influenced her pledging decision.

“I think because I saw other girls like me, that gave me a good gut feeling”

Image: Jazzmin at a sorority social event

Her intuition was on point as, since then, she has formed long-lasting friendships and is “happy to be there, especially in a city where you don’t know anyone”. Although her experience at Plato and AA has been nothing short of remarkable, as she is fully immersed in this student life, Jazzmin is fully aware that she is the only black friend for some of her student association friends, which for Jazzmin is both a gift and a curse.

“As soon as people get to know you, they do not really care about your background”

Though, to make her feel included, Jazzmin has sometimes had to hear that her peers don’t see colour. This dismissal of racial identity may have been done with the right intentions, yet her reaction to this statement was toe-curling. As “colours are there to be seen and not to be ignored ”. Unfortunately, Jazzmin has also experienced these insensitive situations in which stereotypical jokes are made. She assumes that the students who have made these remarks probably find the jokes acceptable, as they are not about her. This has left her to wonder if her presence or the relationships she has formed in the association is the only reason she is not targeted by these jokes.

It goes to say that Jazzmin is fully aware of the controversies surrounding Dutch student associations. That being said, she has been fortunate enough not to be a victim of these tragedies. She is grateful for the opportunities Plato has provided her and the new skills she has developed. Finally, she acknowledges the privilege she has as a black girl in a Dutch student association and hopes to attract a more diverse group of students in the coming years.

Image: Jazzmin at a sorority social event

My Love is Worthy

Contributor: A.A

Love is Love. That's the motto of our community. Yet love, even within our modern times, is still such a controversial term. Being gay and being able to love and be loved is still not allowed and a problem in many countries. As a Persian, I am aware of how socially conservative societies can make it extremely challenging for one to live their truth.

Being bullied, mistreated and discriminated against are a few of the common intolerant attitudes an LGBTQ+ person faces daily. We have to tolerate many things. It could be the weird faces people make when you walk hand in hand with someone else, the insensitive questions other people ask, or if it is the way you have been taught in school that only men and women are allowed to love each other.

Image: LGBT Muslims, Allies Come Together for Pride, David Artavia

It doesn’t even affect me any more if somebody calls me ‘f*ggot“ or comments some bible verse under my Instagram profile telling me how being gay will send me to hell. Not only that, but it is a part of my life, which I was forced to adapt to. Many of our community had to adapt to the hatred of the people and accept it because it is easier than to change those people’s mindset.

For people not in my skin, being gay seems so easy, as they have never been confronted with all the issues that come with the colourful rainbow. Moreover, even within the rainbow, there is still a lot of room for improvement as discrimination and racism also extend to our rows.

Nevertheless, I am thankful for pride month, as it raises awareness about the people who have been mistreated for decades. Pride reminds me that my existence is valid and provides understanding to younger people who still struggle with their identity to understand that the possibility of being different exists. I am looking very forward to the future of our community. I am thankful to know that the coming generations have to face less injustice as previous generations did.

Life at a Rap Label / Music Agency

Contributors:

Chairene Chidozie & Ilaria Obata

Opening Statement

One of the main reasons F-OICE-S aims to provide a feminist lens to critical readings of our contemporary setting is based on the constant interactions with heteronormative and hegemonic structures that women face.

I use the term heteronormative matrix here, as defined by North-America philosopher and gender theorist Judith Butler, as "the metaphor of the matrix is a system of externally imposed rules with an understanding of how the matrix – to the extent that it exists – relies on hegemony as organised consent." Butler refers to the matrix as a fixed hegemonic system between genders, sexuality, and heteronormative tropes surrounding masculinity and femininity. Wherein masculinity has been shaped and moulded based on the power structures that have arisen from centuries of patriarchal lines of governance. With governance here meaning a manner of governing a state, organization, but also the governance of social milieus and relational dynamics.

This sense of patriarchal governance is present within our everyday contextual interactions: from being told that 'you run like a girl', or to 'man up' by forcing one to suppress emotional reactions all together.

In effect, four different women working in the Dutch music industry, within different record labels, shared their own respective experiences as a female in this male-dominated industry. A commonality between some of these interviews was that some women automatically felt that they became set into roles, values, and stereotypical feminine tropes within these spaces. This stems from a long history of women being treated as property to men, and the idea that men can control, dictate, and directly influence how a woman should lead her life.

I believe the anonymous stories submitted to us from women working in a predominantly white, Dutch, male institution correlates towards this notion of how in almost every institutional space, there is a prevailing sense of hegemony based on your own gender identity.

These conversations go further than just speaking about a 'boys club'. They are heavily linked with how historical modes of production, in a capitalist market within our current contextual setting in Western Europe, still stem from the idea that there is a gendered division of labour. In this division, women have constantly been anchored to a relation of servitude, from the 'nuclear family' home, to their depiction across almost every form of mainstream media as either secondarily tied to a main male character, or as this figure of care and nurture. Thus, it is necessary to acknowledge how this historical positionality still interferes with workplace dynamics in our contemporary setting, as the status of these women in this industry is still coated with nature-like inexorability. From being perceived as just an object, a caterer, submissive, quiet and unable to speak out about what they feel is unequal, unjust, and wrong in this industry.

The Future of Young Women in the Record Label Industry

Image: by Cynthia Kitler for BLOOMBERG BUSINESSWEEK,

Although it is undeniable why ensuring equal representation of women in the workplace can change company dynamics for the better, within the music industry in the Netherlands, women remain marginalized through exclusionary career advancing spaces, hostile sexism; including generalized assumptions that any type of musical instrument or sound engineering/sound recording techniques are "too advanced for women".

Hence, the toxic culture that still roams in the record label industry has left four women that we interviewed to wonder whether a job at a record label, their sub-labels or external label service agencies is right for them. The initial consensus among these women was that working at a record label or sub-agency would develop their professional aptitude and lead them to better career opportunities in this industry. Furthermore, getting insight into artists’ work and exclusive access to these spaces also had its attractive qualities. Over time, these views transposed to frustration and general resentment towards the ‘old boys club’ at their workplace. For some of these women, having to deal with bothersome comments daily, often concerning feminine stereotypes, has left them to feel undervalued and often put aside if they were to make a complaint.

Image: by Liberty Antonia Sadler for Metro.co.uk

Furthermore, in fast-paced industries, such as record labels, mistakes are bound to occur, which is why workplaces must embrace failure and grow from them. Unfortunately, for these women, working in this male-dominated industry, the attitude towards failure is pernicious and pervasive. They also stated that it is common for their mistakes to trigger irrational reactions. Moreover, one respondent shared that more leniency is shown towards her male colleagues. On the contrary, their male colleagues’ mistakes are often put off, and due to this ‘enabling’ culture, their mistakes are also less readily admitted. As a result, there is this inequality of discipline, where female employees responsible for trivial to minor missteps are subject to far worse penalties than male colleagues.

This inequality of discipline can often leave them to wonder about their job security. More specifically, whether their job is worth the stress and trauma. The survey we made, showed that there is a correlation between toxic workplace culture and job satisfaction. Highlighting the necessity for these record labels to dedicate time to enacting policies, which ensure and maintain an equal and respectful working environment. Wherein, record labels can demonstrate that gender diversity is a high priority and implement policies that foster an inclusive culture.

Although this may sound impossible considering how white and male most record labels are, Suzi Analogue, producer, composer, and recording artist, decided to found her own record label, Never Normal Records, and the multimedia collective Never Normal Soundsystem in 2014. As stated in her interview with i-D, her aversion to toxic working environments she encountered within most record labels - where she felt tokenized,

“Led me to found Never Normal, a place for kids who grew up listening to everything — multi-talented, brown and black, and making electronica, hip hop, whatever we really want. Because there were only a couple labels doing this, and they were so male. They were just so male! I knew where I stood, I knew what I felt, and I really went with my vibe.”

Never Normal records, thus, provides the space for the possibility to deconstruct these gendered power dynamics within an industry that is deeply misogynistic.

Image: Never Normal Records

Finally, the status of women in the record label industry is obscure. Their voices are often muted, and their demands often disregarded, despite women bringing so much to the table. Furthermore, the history of women in leadership positions at record labels demonstrates how their presence influences change and contributes to profitable returns for the music economy. Therefore, honest conversations about gender inclusion in the workplace must be encouraged, as this may retain female talent in this fast-paced industry.

Thus, part of this ongoing conversation that we are trying to have is also rooted in the inability to contest this gendered power dynamic, for it feels like things won't change, or these voices will never be heard. Yet, F-OICE-S aim to centre these voices to deconstruct hegemonic power structures rooted in gendered divisions across institutional landscapes.
